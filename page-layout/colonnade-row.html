Colonnade Row

My Field Report

Building Basic Information

The Colonnade Row is on Lafayette Street in Manhattan in New York City. This building was built in the 1830s and was built in the Greek Revival form. It was initially built as houses, but now it is used as both houses and commercial spaces. The Colonnade Row used to be wider than today’s scale because some parts of it had been torn down during a relocation. With its noticeable Greek-Revival-style appearance, Colonnade Row has become a landmark on Lafayette Street.

Building Analysis & Sketch

Let’s get to the façade of Colonnade Row. The most observable feature of this building is its Corinthian orders: 13 columns with leaf-like capitals, bases, and fluting equally divide the space of the façade proportionally and rhythmically. Besides, the entablature and frieze are on top of the columns, fulfilling the structure of the orders. However, on its façade, there is no pediment. Behind the columns is the wall and the windows on two different floors. Under the columns, there is still one first floor. The outside wall of the first floor still follows the vertical rhythms of the columns and windows. Even though these columns are the most important elements of Colonnade Row, Colonnade Row uses walls to carry the weight rather than the columns, and these Greek elements are just decorations outside on its façade. Also, the façade of Colonnade Row was built in an ancient Greek Temple style, but it was actually built as houses for people to live.

Colonnade Row was built in the Greek Revival style in 1830s is perhaps because people at that time wanted to show their pursuit of democracy and freedom, which is represented by ancient Greek cultures. More importantly, since it used to be houses, people might want to show that living in Colonnade Row is living with democracy.

Finally, though Colonnade Row is a landmark, it seems it is not protected well: the marble on the façade of the building looks old, weathered, and unkept. All orders and some parts of the metopes are wrapped with papers or nets, perhaps for protection. Colonnade Row might need a complete renewal, and current protection might not be enough.
